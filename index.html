The process of European integration and the EU institutions


What is European integration

European integration is the long process where European countries decided to work together more and more to keep peace and to have a stronger common economy.


Idea and start

After World War II, many leaders wanted cooperation so war would be less likely again. One early step was the Treaty of Brussels (1948), which is often seen as part of the first post-war cooperation in Europe.


Important steps

  • 1951: creation of the European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC) to manage coal and steel together, because these resources were very important for industry.
  • 1957: Treaties of Rome that created the European Economic Community (EEC) and Euratom; cooperation widened toward a common market.
  • 1992: Maastricht Treaty created the European Union and made the project more political, not only economic.
  • 2002: euro banknotes and coins were introduced in countries that use the euro.

How laws are made

EU legislation is produced through a process shared by several bodies:

  • European Commission: prepares new proposals, protects the general interest of the EU and checks that EU rules are applied.
  • European Parliament: elected by citizens; helps make laws and controls the Commission.
  • Council of the European Union: represents national governments (ministers) and adopts laws together with the Parliament.
  • Budget: the Parliament and the Council decide the EU budget together.

Other key bodies

  • European Council: made up of heads of state or government; gives main political direction and big priorities but does not legislate in the ordinary way.
  • Court of Justice of the European Union: ensures EU law is interpreted and applied the same way in all member states.
  • European Court of Auditors: checks how EU money is spent.
  • European Central Bank (ECB): manages monetary policy for the euro area.
